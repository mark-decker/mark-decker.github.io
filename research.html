Research Statement
My approach to scientiﬁc discovery utilizes my knowledge of physical processes, mathe-
matical techniques, and observations to develop, improve, and utilize climate models. My speciﬁc
focus has been on land and land-atmosphere interactions at scales from a point to the globe. My
research stands at the nexus of atmospheric science, climate science, hydrology, and ecology, with
implications for water management, agriculture, and climate and weather prediction. I am a strong
proponent of collaborative studies that involve experts from various research specialties. Weather
and climate models are the key tool utilized to answer questions regarding the future, be it tomor-
row or in decades. Despite the necessity of models as a tool in climate, weather, and water research,
most researchers only use these tools. I develop models including new and innovative numerical
techniques and parameterizations that capture the physical processes that govern the interaction
between the land and the atmosphere. My strategy for using and developing models is to take full
advantage of observations and model-data fusions in traditional and novel ways to gain insight into
these physical processes. My goal is to develop the tool that enable modeling to guide solutions to
important problems in hydrology, agriculture, and environmental management.

After ﬁnishing my PhD I began my ﬁrst post doctoral position at the Climate Change
Research Centre at the University of New South Wales. I brought my expertise of soil mois-
ture dynamics, groundwater, and land surface processes to demonstrate that groundwater is an
essential process that dampens the interannual variability in transpiration ﬂuxes over forests in
South-Eastern Australia. Validating the performance of water ﬂuxes in land surface weather and
climate models is challenging at large scales because no observations of transpiration exist out-
side point measurements. To overcome this deﬁciency in observations, I developed a technique of
using vegetation greenness observations to validate the spatial behavior of transpiration anoma-
lies in semi-arid regions. This novel technique was used to demonstrate how groundwater impacts
inter-annual transpiration anomalies in Eucalypt forests in Australia. Upon starting my work with
the community atmosphere biosphere land exchange (CABLE) land surface model, the model was
developed primarily for single site simulations but used in a global climate model as part of CMIP4
and CMIP5. The point scale heritage of CABLE was evident as it neglected large scale hydrological
processes. I developed a new parameterization for subgrid scale soil moisture, runoﬀ generation,
and groundwater. The parameterizations utilize an assumed subgrid distribution of soil moisture,
that dynamically varies the heterogeneity with soil moisture content, a feature missing in other pa-
rameterizations. The development of state of the art hydrological parameterizations within CABLE
brought the scientiﬁc state of the land model competitive with others internationally.

Current Research
I am currently trying to answer a very simple question ’Can oﬄine physically based sim-
ulations of irrigated croplands properly project irrigation demand?’ While the impact of irrigation
on local climate has been established, simulations of crop yield and irrigation under climate change
use oﬄine models that ignore feedbacks between irrigation changes and the atmosphere. Using cou-
pled and oﬄine simulations, I have found that the irrigation demand cannot be calculated without
taking into account the local feedbacks on the planetary boundary layer. The international push
for including water management in models has neglected this important process. The future use of
land models to calculate irrigation amounts under future climates will be fundamentally impacted
by this discovery.
I am also spending considerable time implementing the land modeling advances I have
achieved in the Australian Community Climate model (ACCESS). The current generation of the
model performs inadequately when coupled to a land surface scheme that properly simulates the
hydrological cycle due to existing model biases. While my colleagues upgrade the atmospheric
component of the model I am updating the groundwater, runoﬀ, and soil evaporation advances to
work with this new version. I am also collaborating with colleagues at the University of Arizona
to further develop the soil evaporation model for the use in the National Center for Atmospheric
Research Climate Model. Once completed this work will result in a very rich vein of potential
research, allowing me to explore how soil moisture, runoﬀ, and evaporation processes limit and

Future Research
The overarching goal of my future research is to develop and utilize models in combination
with observations to solve grand challenges at the intersection of climate change, water resources,
and agriculture. To achieve this goal I will advance the understanding of hydrology and land-
atmosphere interactions in a team I will build within the department, work closely with the scientists
within the department whose expertise complement my own, and work closely with national and
international research centers.
One area I will pursue will build on my current research that demonstrates the fundamental
deﬁciency with forecasting irrigation demand using land only simulations. Irrigation accounts for
40% of the worlds total food product; seasonal and decadal forecasts of irrigation demand are vital
to society to ensure equitable water allocation. The CMIP5 climate model from the National Center
for Atmospheric Research, cannot adequately capture the atmospheric feedbacks on surface ﬂuxes of
water and will be a top priority for my immediate research. I will ensure more accurate projections
and forecasts of irrigation demand on long timescale. In a similar vein, observational analysis
demonstrate that the heterogeneity of soil moisture on scales much smaller than the resolution of
climate models drives secondary circulations resulting in clouds and precipitation. These processes
are explicitly included only when the model resolution is smaller than the scale of the secondary
circulations. Climate models also neglect these feedbacks and therefore cannot accurately simulate
land-atmosphere feedbacks. This area is ripe for disruption by extending the parameterizations of
soil moisture heterogeneity I have developed to include representations of these processes. Including
these processes is diﬃcult because they are not fully resolved. I will use high resolution atmospheric
models to derive a statistical relationship between the unresolved soil moisture heterogeneity in
climate models and the vertical ﬂuxes of heat and moisture in the boundary layer. This approach
has previously been used for deriving convective parameterizations and will follow a similar method
to deduce boundary layer heat ﬂuxes from surface moisture variations.
